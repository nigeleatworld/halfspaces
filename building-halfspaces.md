# Building halfspaces

I know almost nothing about website building; I don't have a designer's eye in the trained sense, and I don't know the proper vocabulary for half the things I react to. My wardrobe is a mixture of white or grey tees, five comfortable-fitting yoga pants from the same brand, and oversized retro jerseys; I'm the last person who'd know what tasteful design looks like. I can look at a page and say "this feels cramped" or "too corporate" or "that colour feels wrong" without always being able to explain what rule has been violated.

In May 2026 I tried to launch a personal website. I owned `halfspaces.co`, I'd updated my resume, and if a hiring manager clicked the link and found nothing there, that would undercut the resume itself. The resume kept using words like *build*, *systems*, *AI*, *data*, *productised insight*; the claim that I built useful things around messy problems. If the site was empty, all of that stayed a claim.

So the job of the site became clear: it had to be proof. Not perfect proof, not a polished agency-grade portfolio; a proof-of-work layer.

The rest was messier than expected.

## A resume companion

The site began as an extension of the resume; the resume was one page, compressing seniority, market research experience, leadership, AI curiosity, and builder energy. A website could breathe.

At first I thought it might be a clean personal landing page: name, intro, projects, contact link. Too generic, the kind of page that could have belonged to anyone.

The site needed to carry one idea: *a market researcher with 13 years of experience, now using AI to turn ideas into interfaces*. That became the hero:

> I'm a market researcher with 13 years of experience, now using AI to turn my ideas into interfaces. This site is my proof-of-work layer: a place to show how I think, and what I've built.

The phrase "proof-of-work layer" was the line that made it click; the site wasn't a portfolio, it was evidence.

## About the name

The name had to make sense in the first scroll.

In geometry, a half-space is one side of a boundary: a region made clear by a constraint. In football, the half-space is the inside channel between the centre and the wing; the kind of space creative players often occupy; not the most crowded, not the widest, tactically valuable, close enough to hurt and quiet enough to receive.

I tried longer versions; the geometry line was short, the football line ran on. The final pair:

> In geometry: one side of a boundary; a region made clear by a constraint.
> In football: the inside channel between centre and wing; where creative play often resides.

There's a small diagram at the top: not a full pitch, just a rectangle with two horizontal shaded bands and a dot. It connects the two definitions without explaining either.

## The hero was the hardest part

The hero took the most back-and-forth, which surprised me; I'd thought the project sections would be harder.

Most of the work happened mornings at Ya Kun, after dropping Brennan at infant care; laptop on the table, kopi c siu dai peng in a takeaway cup, and back to the file.

The first screen has to answer too many questions at once: who are you, what is this, why should I keep scrolling, what is the tone, are you serious, are you trying too hard.

Early versions felt too generic. Then too corporate, too sparse, too cramped; sometimes too sparse and too cramped in the same draft. The categories of wrong kept multiplying.

Eventually the hero settled into: the halfspaces definition, a life-in-years dot strip, "Hello! Nigel here," the positioning line, action links, and a small post-it card listing the five threads.

The dot strip was a late addition; or mid-process, I keep calling it late. Ninety dots, thirty-nine filled to represent my age, the last filled dot blinking subtly to show I'm 39 and alive, the last 13 marked separately as "13 years in market research."

## Design is mostly saying "not that"

I reacted negatively to versions long before I could articulate why: too corporate, too grey, too jarring. Too much empty space; then too cramped, sometimes within the same hour. Too "AI slop," too self-deprecating, too LinkedIn. Too polished in a way that didn't feel like me.

At first that felt inefficient; it was the design process. I didn't have the vocabulary upfront, so I discovered it through rejections, and every "not that" narrowed the field.

Each of the five project sections had to answer the same two questions: what is it, and why did I build it. Earlier versions mixed those with "what exists now," which made some projects feel more real than others when the difference was just maturity. Status moved into metadata: *Beta, Live, In use, Idea, Pre-launch*.

Introspace was the hardest visually. The first version of its teletext panel had a black background with bright yellow, blue, red, cyan, green; it worked as a reference, it didn't work next to the rest of the site. I muted it.

Colour was the clearest example. Or maybe spacing; they ran in parallel, and colour was the one I could finally explain. The first version had one gradient and then snapped back to white; the sections now bleed tonally through warm white, parchment, sage, blue-grey, aged yellow, clay, and a dark-blue closing panel.

The photo question came back several times: for the resume, no photo; for the website, yes. I tried it in the hero and it never worked; one version had my face planted huge on the hero, which I will not be sharing. The photo moved to the closing contact card, where, by the time someone gets there, it isn't decorative; it anchors the page.

The closing line was rewritten several times. An earlier version was *"Define the space. Build inside it."* It sounded elegant, and it also implied I only build inside predefined boxes; the wrong message for a portfolio. I eventually went with Einstein:

> "Life is like riding a bicycle. To keep your balance, you must keep moving."

## What AI did not do

AI helped me build the site, obviously; what I noticed more was what AI didn't do.

It didn't know what sounded like me. It generated "AI frontier" for the hero a dozen times before I worked out why it kept making me wince; too LinkedIn. It knew the Introspace visual was conceptually right; it had no way of knowing it was visually too much next to everything around it. It didn't catch that *"Define the space. Build inside it."* carried a subtle negative implication.

AI made iteration cheap; taste still had to come from somewhere. Taste came from a lot of imperfect reactions: *not that, closer, too much, too little, this feels more like me*; that loop was the work.

## Shipping

Before launch, no one had seen the site. Amanda had been pleased with the name from early on, which helped; everything else I'd kept to myself.

The deployment was simple, once I stopped looking for the "Create" button that wasn't there. I committed to GitHub, the repo was already connected to Cloudflare Pages, and Cloudflare detected the push and redeployed.

The site is live; that's not the same as being done.

I started this because I had a domain and an empty page, and I thought I'd need to learn design and code. What I needed was the willingness to keep saying *"not that"* until what was left felt true. The AI handled the generation; the work was rejection; the vocabulary came after.
