# I Thought I Was Building a Website. I Was Really Building a Point of View.

I know almost nothing about website building.

That is not false modesty. I do not have a designer's eye in the trained sense. I do not know the proper vocabulary for half the things I react to. I can look at a page and say, "this feels cramped," or "this feels too corporate," or "that colour feels wrong," but I cannot always explain, at least at first, what rule has been violated.

And yet, sometime in May 2026, I found myself trying to launch a personal website.

Not because I suddenly wanted to become a front-end developer. Not because I had some elaborate personal branding strategy. The reason was simpler, and slightly more urgent: I had updated my resume, I was preparing to send it out, and I owned `halfspaces.co`.

If a hiring manager saw the link, clicked it, and found nothing there, that would be odd.

Worse, it would undermine the thing I was trying to say about myself.

My resume kept using words like "build," "systems," "AI," "data," "research," "productised insight." I was trying to position myself not just as someone who has worked in market research for 13 years, but as someone who builds useful things around messy problems.

If the website was empty, all of that would remain a claim.

So the job of the website became clear enough:

It had to be proof.

Not perfect proof. Not a polished agency-grade portfolio. But enough of a proof-of-work layer for someone to see that I am not just talking about building. I am actually trying to build.

That was the start.

The rest was much messier than I expected.

## The Website Started As A Resume Companion

The website did not begin as a grand creative project.

It began as an extension of my resume.

Before this, I had been working on the resume itself. There were decisions about whether to include a photo, how much whitespace was enough, whether the bullets were too tight, whether the intro sounded like me, whether "I build and run research functions" was stronger than "I'm a quantitative researcher by trade and builder by instinct."

That resume process mattered because it created the raw positioning for the site.

The resume was one page. It had to be compressed. It had to signal seniority, market research experience, leadership, AI curiosity, and builder energy without becoming bloated.

But a website could breathe.

A website could show the things the resume could only point at.

So the question became: what should `halfspaces.co` do?

At first, I thought it might just be a clean personal landing page. Name, intro, projects, maybe a contact link.

But that was too generic. It could have belonged to anyone.

The more we worked on it, the more I realised that the site needed to carry one singular idea:

I am a market researcher with 13 years of experience, now using AI to turn ideas into interfaces.

That line eventually became the backbone of the hero:

> I'm a market researcher with 13 years of experience, now using AI to turn my ideas into interfaces. This site is my proof-of-work layer: a place to show how I think, and what I've built.

That did not arrive fully formed. It came after many versions that felt too polished, too "LinkedIn," too generic, too vague, or simply not enough like me.

The phrase "proof-of-work layer" mattered. It made the site less like a portfolio and more like evidence.

## The Name Had To Earn Its Place

One unexpectedly important part of the process was explaining the name: halfspaces.

At first, the site had the name, but the meaning was not obvious. That created a disconnect. If someone landed on `halfspaces.co`, why was it called that?

We explored two definitions.

In geometry, a half-space is one side of a boundary, a region made clear by a constraint.

In football, the half-space is the inside channel between the centre and the wing. It is the kind of space creative players often occupy. Not the most crowded area, not the widest area, but a tactically valuable channel where creative play can start.

That football meaning mattered to me because it had a certain beauty. The half-space is not just "space." It is useful space. It is close enough to hurt, quiet enough to receive, and often where creative play begins.

At one point the definition became too long and lopsided. The geometry explanation was short, the football explanation was a paragraph. That felt visually and conceptually wrong. We kept tightening until the final top definition became:

> In geometry: one side of a boundary; a region made clear by a constraint.  
> In football: the inside channel between centre and wing; where creative play often resides.

That was enough.

There is also a small logo-like diagram at the top. It is not a full football pitch. It is a rectangle with horizontal shaded spaces and a dot, meant to connect the geometry and football ideas without over-explaining them.

That was one of the first lessons: if your site has a name that is not immediately obvious, the explanation cannot feel like an appendix. It has to be part of the experience.

## The Hero Was The Hardest Part

The hero section took the most back-and-forth.

That surprised me. I thought the hard part would be the project sections. It turned out the hardest part was the first screen.

The first screen has to answer several questions at once:

Who are you?

What is this?

Why should I keep scrolling?

What is the tone?

Are you serious?

Are you trying too hard?

The early versions felt too generic. Then too corporate. Then too sparse. Then too cramped. Then too much like a design template.

We tried a lot.

There was a version with top navigation. We removed it because, for now, it served no purpose.

There was a version where the hero had too much unused space. We rebalanced it.

There was a version where "halfspaces" wrapped awkwardly. That had to go.

There was a version where the intro sounded like a professional bio. I did not want that. I wanted something closer to: welcome, here are my attempts at being creative and solving real-world problems, enjoy your stay.

Eventually the hero settled into a few parts:

- the halfspaces definition at the top
- a "life in years" dot strip
- a 13-year market research marker
- name, location, and date
- the main "Hello! Nigel here."
- positioning: market researcher / product builder / AI explorer
- the proof-of-work intro
- action links: open to work, threads, CV, LinkedIn, email
- a post-it style card listing the five proof-of-work threads

The dot strip was a late addition, but it gave the page a bit of personality.

It shows 90 dots. Thirty-nine are filled, representing my age. The final dot blinks subtly, to show that I am 39 and alive. The last 13 dots are marked separately as "13 years in market research."

That was a small detail, but it made the page feel more mine. It also turned a CV fact into a visual element.

The "Open to work" button was another delicate decision. It had to be visible without being desperate. The final version is a small, calm green badge. It says the thing clearly, without shouting.

## The Projects Had To Prove The Word "Build"

The site eventually centred on five threads:

1. SpacePod
2. Mr. B
3. Budget Dashboard
4. Introspace
5. Bitpybit

Each one had to answer: what is it, and why did I build it?

That sounds obvious, but we had to work through inconsistencies.

At one point, some projects had "What it is" and "Why I built it," while others had "What exists now." That felt uneven. It also made some projects feel more real than others, even when the differences were simply about maturity.

Eventually we standardised everything to:

- What it is
- Why I built it

The current state is carried elsewhere, in the metadata: Beta, Live, In use, Idea, Pre-launch.

That was cleaner.

It also made the sections more balanced. Every thread got the same explanatory structure, even if the underlying projects were at different stages.

## SpacePod: Listening As Searchable Memory

SpacePod is a live podcast knowledge base.

The problem was simple: I would listen to useful podcast episodes and then forget too much of what was said. SpacePod is an attempt to make listening reusable.

The write-up became:

> I'd listen to podcasts and forget a lot of what was said. SpacePod helps keep the wisdom within reach.

What it is:

> A podcast knowledge base: followed shows become transcripts, transcripts become searchable memory, and search becomes cited synthesis.

Why I built it:

> Most knowledge tools treat listening as disposable. I wanted a place where good conversations could be queried later, with sources attached.

At first, we considered using screenshots. Later, we moved toward recreating the interface as an HTML artifact. That made the page feel more consistent and less dependent on static screenshots.

The SpacePod visual shows a library, an answer card, a cited source, and an input field. It is not fully interactive, but it communicates the product idea quickly.

## Mr. B: An Assistant That Nudges

Mr. B is a personal AI assistant, or chief-of-staff type agent, set up to help move the right things forward.

The naming came from my wife. That detail stayed because it humanised the project.

The copy became:

> I wanted an assistant to help me move the right things forward. My wife named him.

What it is:

> A personal AI chief-of-staff for calendar scheduling, daily briefings, nudges, and recurring work.

Why I built it:

> Agentic AI becomes interesting when it is attached to real recurring work, real constraints, and real consequences.

The visual here was tricky.

We wanted it to look like a Telegram chat because Mr. B operates through Telegram. Early versions had unrealistic buttons like "Done," "Snooze," and "Explain," which did not feel like a real chat. We refined it into a more realistic Telegram-style conversation:

Me: "Morning. What needs moving today?"

Mr. B: "Three nudges today. Nothing invented."

Then a list of nudges.

Then a snooze request.

Then Mr. B confirming he will bring it back later.

This section mattered because it shows agentic AI not as a vague buzzword, but as something attached to actual personal workflow.

## Budget Dashboard: Private But Useful

The Budget Dashboard was a later addition.

At first, I was unsure whether to show it because it is personal. But a sanitised dashboard made sense. It was a strong example of turning messy exports and personal finance data into something usable.

The lead became:

> I built a dashboard that tracks every transaction and monitors my financial health.

What it is:

> A private dashboard that turns messy exports into trends, subscriptions, ranked spend, runway, and net-worth views.

Why I built it:

> Money is easier to manage when the system shows what moved, what matters, and what decisions are available next.

There was a design issue here too. The screenshot approach made the dashboard feel too large and awkward. So it became a recreated dashboard artifact: category trend cards, sparklines, ranked spend.

That made it fit the rest of the site better.

## Introspace: Trust-Based Referrals As A Workflow

Introspace was the most visually difficult.

The idea is a private professional network for trust-based referrals. It is still in ideation, not shipped.

The copy:

> I wanted to make the invisible work around referrals more explicit: context, trust, fit, and the right to say no.

What it is:

> A private professional network for people in transition and connectors willing to make trust-based referrals.

Why I built it:

> Cold applications flatten people. Good introductions carry context, but most tools do not structure that context well.

The visual was deliberately inspired by old teletext-like interfaces. That was intentional. It was meant to feel less like another clean SaaS dashboard and more like a private, slightly weird, old-school network.

But it was also too jarring.

The first versions were black, bright yellow, bright blue, red, cyan, green. It worked conceptually, but visually it punched too hard compared with the rest of the site.

So we muted it. Still retro, still teletext, but less neon. Smaller. More controlled. Less likely to dominate the page.

That was a good example of a recurring design lesson: an idea can be right and still need to be toned down.

## Bitpybit: Small Learning Loops

Bitpybit is an iPhone learning app for Python practice.

The lead:

> Bitpybit is deliberately small: one to three Python questions a day, short explanations, and a habit loop that does not ask for too much.

What it is:

> A notification-first iPhone learning app for daily bite-sized Python practice.

Why I built it:

> I wanted to test whether programming practice could feel light enough to survive a normal day.

The visual is an interactive mini quiz in the browser. This was one of the more satisfying parts because it made the site feel alive. You can answer a question and get feedback.

Not every artifact needs to be fully interactive, but having at least one interactive element helped the site feel less like a static brochure.

## Design Is Mostly Saying "Not That"

One of the more interesting parts of the process was how often I reacted negatively before I could articulate what I wanted.

Too corporate.

Too grey.

Too jarring.

Too cramped.

Too much empty space.

Too generic.

Too "AI slop."

Too much like a template.

Too self-deprecating.

Too LinkedIn.

Too polished in a way that did not feel like me.

At first, that felt inefficient. But I think that was actually the design process.

I did not have the vocabulary upfront, so I had to discover it through reactions. Every "not that" narrowed the field.

The site slowly became more opinionated because I kept rejecting things that felt false.

For example, the closing section originally had a line like:

> Define the space. Build inside it.

It sounded elegant, and it connected to the halfspaces idea. But something about it felt off. It had a slightly negative implication, as if I was saying I only build inside predefined boxes.

We changed the closing through-line a few times.

At one point, it became:

> Find the edge. Build the useful thing.

Later, I wanted something closer to movement, learning, and not falling behind. I had been thinking about quotes like "if you're not moving, you're falling behind," but I did not want something that sounded like hustle culture.

Eventually, we used the Einstein quote:

> "Life is like riding a bicycle. To keep your balance, you must keep moving."

That felt better. It connected to the underlying reason I was building the site in the first place.

The tools are moving quickly. I would rather stay close to them, use them properly, and learn by building with them.

That is more honest than pretending I have the whole AI thing figured out.

## The Colour System Took Longer Than Expected

Colours were another rabbit hole.

We started with warm whites, navy, muted blues, sage, clay, post-it yellow. At one point I sent a colour reference image with shades like white, grey-blue, dark navy, muted blue.

Then we tried to build a more confident palette.

The issue was that the site initially had one gradient, and then it snapped back to white. That felt strange. If colour was going to be part of the experience, it needed to move through the page more thoughtfully.

So the sections now bleed tonally:

- the hero starts in clean warm white
- SpacePod moves into parchment
- Mr. B moves into sage
- Budget moves into blue-grey
- Introspace moves into aged yellow
- Bitpybit moves into clay
- the closing section becomes a dark blue panel

The goal was not to make the site colourful for its own sake. The goal was to give the page a sense of progression.

Different projects, different tonal moods, one coherent system.

There were two palette options. One was more "field notebook": warm paper, deep navy ink, slate blue-grey, olive/sage, clay, softened yellow. The other was warmer and more organic, almost atelier-like.

We preferred the field notebook direction because it felt more employable and authoritative. The atelier version was nice, but a little too soft.

That is another thing I learned: colour is not just taste. It changes the perceived seriousness of the page.

## Spacing Was Not Decoration

Spacing became a surprisingly large part of the conversation.

At one point, we removed too much dead space and the hero felt cramped. Then we added back air. Then the gap between the definition and the hero felt too large. Then the first proof section arrived too abruptly. Then the project sections needed consistent visual space.

I kept saying things like "give it more air to breathe," which is not a precise design instruction, but it was the correct instinct.

The lesson for me was that whitespace is not emptiness. It is pacing.

Too little space and the page feels anxious.

Too much space and the page feels unfinished.

The right amount of space makes the reader feel guided.

We also had to make the prototype visuals proportionate. Mr. B's Telegram phone initially took up too much vertical space. Introspace was too loud. SpacePod and Budget needed to sit at a wider artifact scale. Bitpybit and Mr. B needed to remain phone-sized.

The goal was not identical dimensions. The goal was equal visual weight.

That distinction matters.

## The Photo Question Came Back More Than Once

We considered photo vs no photo several times.

For the resume, the answer was no photo. It was not necessary and could introduce bias.

For the website, the answer was different.

The site is personal. It is about proof-of-work, but also about the person behind the work. The photo made sense, especially at the end.

We tried photo treatments in the hero. We tried removing the background. We tried blending it into the background. We tried using the photo more subtly. Some versions looked bad. One cutout looked especially wrong because parts of the top were erased.

Eventually, the photo moved to the closing contact card.

That made more sense.

By the time someone reaches the end, the photo is not decorative. It anchors the final CTA: this is the person, here is how to reach him, here is the CV.

The photo has a job.

## CTAs Need A Reason To Exist

At one point, there were too many email and LinkedIn links.

Email in the hero identity line. Email in the hero actions. Email in the contact card. Email in the footer. LinkedIn in multiple places. It felt repetitive.

So we counted them.

Before cleanup, email appeared too many times. LinkedIn too.

We reduced the CTAs to two intentional places:

1. The hero action row
2. The bottom photo/contact card

We added the CV link in both places too.

That made the contact structure cleaner:

At the top: if you are interested immediately, you can act.

At the bottom: if you have read the page and want to follow up, the contact card is there.

The footer no longer needed email, LinkedIn, or a "built in Singapore" line. It now just has a simple "Top" link.

This was a small UX lesson: repeated CTAs can feel helpful, but they can also feel noisy if they do not have a reason to be there.

## AI Did A Lot, But It Did Not Make The Decisions

The obvious part of this story is that AI helped me build the site.

But the more interesting part is what AI did not do.

It did not decide what sounded like me.

It did not know when the design felt too corporate.

It did not know that "AI frontier" sounded too LinkedIn.

It did not know that the Introspace visual was conceptually right but visually too jarring.

It did not know that "Define the space. Build inside it." had a subtle negative implication.

It did not know when the site had enough breathing room.

It generated options. It implemented changes. It explained tradeoffs. It helped me move quickly. But I still had to react, reject, choose, and refine.

That was the most useful part of the process.

AI made iteration cheap. But taste still had to come from somewhere.

In this case, taste came from a lot of imperfect reactions:

Not that.

Closer.

Too much.

Too little.

This feels more like me.

That loop was the work.

## Deployment Was Less Dramatic Than Expected

The actual deployment was, in the end, simple.

We committed the site to GitHub:

`Redesign halfspaces landing page`

The repo was connected to Cloudflare Pages.

Cloudflare detected the push.

The project already existed under Workers & Pages, connected to `nigeleatworld/halfspaces`, with `halfspaces.co` as a route.

At first, I was looking for "Create" and saw "Create application," which was confusing. But it turned out I did not need to create a new application. The Pages project was already there. The latest push had triggered a deployment.

That was another small lesson: once GitHub and Cloudflare are connected, deployment becomes less of an event and more of a workflow.

Make change. Commit. Push. Cloudflare redeploys.

This matters because the site is not done.

It is live, but not done.

That feels right.

## What I Actually Learned

I still know very little about website building.

But I know more than I did at the start.

I learned that the hard part is not the HTML. The hard part is deciding what the page is for.

I learned that a personal site is not just a container for information. It is a surface for judgment.

I learned that design is not making things pretty. It is making choices visible.

I learned that spacing changes the emotional pace of a page.

I learned that colour changes the seriousness of a page.

I learned that consistency is harder than beauty.

I learned that a screenshot is sometimes worse than a simplified artifact.

I learned that the same line can be technically clear and emotionally wrong.

I learned that CTAs should earn their place.

I learned that "proof of work" only works if the work is actually shown.

I learned that AI can make you faster, but it cannot tell you what feels true.

Most of all, I learned that building a personal website forces a kind of clarity.

You have to decide what you are claiming.

You have to decide what evidence supports that claim.

You have to decide what to leave out.

You have to decide how polished is polished enough.

And eventually, you have to ship.

## The Part I Did Not Expect

The most surprising part was that launching the site changed my relationship to the work.

Before launch, the website was a file. Something local. Something private. Something endlessly adjustable.

After launch, it became a public surface.

That does not mean it is final. In fact, the opposite. Now that it is live, it is easier to treat it as a living thing.

If SpacePod improves, the site should update.

If Mr. B becomes more useful, the site should update.

If Introspace moves from idea to build, the site should update.

If Bitpybit ships, the site should update.

The site is not a monument. It is a workbench.

That is probably the right metaphor.

Not a portfolio of perfect things. Not a polished museum. A public workbench.

A place to show what I am testing, making, and slowly improving.

## Ending

I started this process thinking I needed a website because I had a domain and an empty page.

I ended it with a better understanding of what I wanted the website to prove.

That feels like the real output.

Not just `halfspaces.co`.

A clearer point of view.

The tools are moving quickly. I would rather stay close to them, use them properly, and learn by building with them.

And if life is like riding a bicycle, then this was one small way to keep moving.
