Build journal

Building chatpulse

Five weeks building a system that reads everything I pay to read, and tells me what's worth my attention.

7 May – 12 June 2026 · built with Claude · ~16 working sessions · runs on a Mac Mini under my desk

I pay for more investing research than I can read. A handful of independent writers I trust, each with a daily member chat that never stops, articles that arrive without warning, and one Asian-equities publication with its own busy group. The value was real and I was wasting it; the edge I was paying for went on the chore of keeping up, and the one line that mattered always seemed to get said at 2am in a thread I'd already scrolled past.

So the objective was narrow and a little greedy: distilled, trustworthy investment signal from a select set of sources I already trust, with the noise removed and the conviction kept. Not stock tips, not a recommendation engine; a daily read of what these specific voices are converging on, how strongly they mean it, and who's quietly accumulating what. Replace the unwinnable habit of reading everything with a system that reads everything and hands me the shortlist.

I'm not a developer. I'm a market researcher; thirteen years of turning messy human signal into something a business can act on, which turns out to be most of what this is. I built it across about five weeks of mornings and late nights, with Claude doing the typing and me doing the deciding. This is the real record of that, dates and dead-ends included, because the deciding was the actual work and I think it's worth showing.

How to read this It's a journal, not a tidy after-the-fact story. The architecture diagrams are here, but so are the days I was confidently wrong. The interesting parts were almost never the code.

The shape, before the story

One picture first, so the dates have something to hang on. Data comes in, gets understood, goes out; the whole system is that sentence, and every hard decision lives in the word "understood."

in → understood → out. the whole thing in one line; the rest is detail.
7 MayThursday · week 1 · day 1

Reading the firehose

The first thing to solve isn't intelligence, it's plumbing; you can't analyse what you can't reach. Day one was a scraper that pulls posts and comments out of a paid chat into a local database. Boring, and the right place to start, because everything downstream is only as good as what lands here.

The first lesson arrived almost immediately and it was a human one, not a technical one: about half the people in a chat have no public profile, so you can know what was said and never who said it. I burned a while trying to resolve identities before accepting that the gap was structural, not a bug. A recurring theme started here; a lot of the work is learning what the data refuses to give you, and designing around the refusal.

11 MayMonday · week 1

From one source to many, and getting told off by a server

By the second week it had to read everyone, not one chat; same database, several publications, both their chats and their article archives. The backfill pulled in thousands of posts and a hundred-odd thousand comments, which is when the politeness problem showed up.

Their servers rate-limit you, and not the way you'd guess; bursting into a fresh source right after a long run on a shared endpoint trips a "too many requests" wall even when you've been deliberately slow. Several runs failed before I taught it to back off, honour the server's "wait this long" header, and treat a payment-required response as a polite skip rather than a crash. The instinct to go faster is exactly wrong here; the only run that matters long-term is the small daily one, and slow-and-unbothered beats fast-and-blocked.

Lesson Don't parallelise the scraper to feel clever. The data's owners can see you, and the whole thing only works if it looks like a person quietly reading what they pay for.
12–13 MayTue–Wed · week 1

The actual hard part: deciding what counts as a signal

This is where it stopped being plumbing. A chat is mostly noise; "gm", jokes, screenshots, and people asking what everyone thinks of a name. Out of that, I wanted only the lines where a person is genuinely taking a position, tagged with how much they meant it. Getting a model to do that reliably is less about prompting and more about defining, precisely, the thing you're looking for.

The rules that took the longest to pin down were all about restraint. A reader asking "thoughts on this one?" mentions a company while holding no view of it; that's not a signal. "There'll always be a buyer for these" is an observation, not a stance. A pasted news headline is the news's opinion, not the author's, unless they say otherwise. Each of these is obvious to a human and slippery to a model, and each one is a rule I had to write and then defend against the system's eagerness to find meaning in everything.

every item, read the same way, reduced to the same handful of fields — or to nothing

I built the example set the model learns from by hand, and kept red-lining it with Claude until the edge cases were covered. The output is deliberately strict and boring: a fixed set of fields, an exact quote for every claim, and an empty answer when there's nothing real. Silence had to be a first-class result. A system that stays quiet is useful; one that invents conviction to fill space is worse than nothing.

14–17 Mayweek 2

Choosing the cheap brain on purpose

Reading every message with a frontier model would be lovely and would also quietly bleed money. So I ran a bake-off; the same few-shot examples against a flagship model and a much cheaper one, graded by hand. The cheap one agreed with the expensive one about ninety-odd percent of the time, at a small fraction of the cost, and its failures leaned the safe way; it under-read rather than hallucinated. Easy call. The expensive model gets saved for the single thing a human actually reads each day; the morning synthesis. Everything else, the millions of tiny reads, runs cheap.

spend the money where a person is looking; nowhere else
17–19 Mayweek 2

Turning signals into something I'd actually open

Now there were signals in a database; useless until shaped into a read. The brief got a fixed structure: what's new, what's converging across sources, the blunt action calls, the early first-mentions, the risks. And one rule that does most of the work; rank by agreement, not by volume. Counting mentions just surfaces whatever's loudest. What I care about is the quieter event of several independent writers landing on the same name, the same way, around the same time.

That forced a distinction I now think is the heart of the whole thing: whose voice is it. The people who write the publications are one tier; their own calls are what count toward conviction. Everyone else in the chat is a second tier, kept as colour and early warning, never quietly promoted into "the author said so." Conflating those two is the single easiest way to make a signal feed lie to you.

whose voice it is matters as much as what they said

By the end of week two it was delivering a real brief to a chat on my phone each morning. It looked finished. It wasn't.

20 MayWednesday · the long day

The day everything came back half-written

This is my favourite entry, because it's the most honest about how building actually goes. The brief kept feeling thin on exactly the long, meaty articles that should have mattered most. The signals were there for short posts and missing for the big pieces, which made no sense until I looked at what was actually landing in the database: the long paid articles were arriving truncated, cut off right where the paywall sits, even though I was sending a valid paid login.

What followed was a couple of hours of pure detective work; Claude and I throwing probe after probe at it. Was the login being ignored? No. Was it a redirect? No. At one point the culprit looked like a compression setting quietly mangling the response, which was a real red herring I chased for a while. The actual answer was that one endpoint serves a polite truncated preview to everyone, and a different, less obvious endpoint serves the full body to a paid session. Switch endpoints, re-pull everything, problem gone.

the system wasn't broken; I was reading the wrong door

The same day I also tightened the extraction rules twice more, the second time to forbid a specific failure I'd caught: the model inventing a plausible ticker for a thesis when the author never named one. Its training data is full of plausible guesses; left unchecked it will confidently supply one. Better to miss a real signal than to manufacture a fake.

23 MaySaturday

A wall I'd built for myself

I'd written a neat way for my personal assistant agent to query all of this directly. Then I remembered the agent doesn't live on the same machine as the data; it runs on its own isolated box. You can't reach a database on one computer by pointing at a file path on another. The clever thing I'd built simply couldn't work, and the honest move was to write that down, mark it not-deployable, and sketch the real fix for later rather than pretend. It sat unsolved for almost two weeks.

4 JuneThursday · week 4, after living with it

Grading my own homework

After a fortnight of actually receiving the daily brief, I stopped and audited it properly; ten briefs read against what the system could have known. The test I trust most was a ground-truth one: I browsed the sources myself, wrote down three things I'd have wanted flagged, then traced each through the pipeline to see where it was won or lost. All three were sitting in the database. None had surfaced cleanly.

The verdict was clarifying and a little humbling: the reading and understanding were fine; the last step, deciding what to show, was the weak link. The worst offender was the risk section quietly attributing a community member's offhand quip to a named author. Good thing to catch, since misattribution in a trust-based feed is close to the worst failure it can have. I fixed it, then built the thing the audit told me I actually needed; a live layer.

4–6 Juneweek 4

From a morning newspaper to a wire

The single daily brief was good for "what did I miss overnight" and bad for "something is happening now." So it grew an intraday layer that stays silent unless an event actually fires; a writer opens a position, a quiet name suddenly heats up across several voices, a new article lands. The morning brief stayed as the orienting read; the live nudges sit on top for urgency. The rule I held to was that a pure schedule ("nothing new, here's your 3pm ping") trains you to ignore the feed, so it only ever speaks when there's a reason.

I also spent a session teaching it two things it kept getting subtly wrong; that "new position" means someone just bought, the single most newsworthy fact in a chat, and that "that short worked out nicely" is a closed trade being remembered fondly, not a live bearish call. Both are the difference between a stance and a memory, and both are exactly the kind of judgement the model won't make unless you make it explicit.

7 JuneSaturday

Giving it a second mouth

Two weeks after admitting the cross-machine wall, I built the real fix: a small, locked-down query service on the machine that holds the data, reachable only over my private network, that answers questions instead of handing over files. Now I can ask back. "Why is this name showing up?" returns who said what, in their own words, even for names too thinly covered to ever make the morning brief. The brief pushes what it decided mattered; this lets me pull everything it didn't. That push-and-pull pairing is, I think, when it stopped being a report and started being a tool.

the report became a tool the day I could interrogate it
9 JuneMonday · a detour that was my fault

Confidently wrong about my own desk

Connecting the assistant to the query service should have been quick. It turned into an evening, and the cause was me. I'd got it into my head that the assistant's machine was somehow a separate, untrusted thing, so Claude and I went down a careful, fiddly path of sharing one machine across two private networks, with access-control rules and the works. It half-worked in a way that's worse than not working; the connection would say it was reachable, then quietly refuse on the one port that mattered, because that setup needs permission on both networks and I'd only granted it on one.

The thing is, both machines are mine. They sit on the same desk. The moment I said that out loud, the whole elaborate solution evaporated; just put the second machine on my own network like the others, done. There was a smaller comedy inside it too, where the login kept silently signing in as the wrong account until we forced a fresh browser window. The two of us, Claude and me, both capable of real work, spending an hour solving a problem I'd invented by misremembering where my own computers live.

The pattern Almost none of my errors were the code being hard. They were me being confidently slightly wrong about something I should have known, and then both of us building cleverly on top of the wrong premise. The fix was usually to say the obvious thing out loud.
11–12 Juneweek 5

The last source, and a flag I forgot

The final piece was folding in the Asian-equities publication, which was awkward because it lives on a different platform and has its member chat on a different one again; articles fetched one way, chat read another, both needing to land as a single voice so they'd converge together. That part went smoothly. The part that didn't, again, was me.

I spent a chunk of the night convinced of a deep data-loss bug; a backfill kept reporting it had processed two hundred items while saving exactly none. I read the code three times and even checked whether two processes were fighting over the database. The cause was a single flag I'd left off my own command, the one that says "actually write this down." It had been doing precisely what I asked, which was a dry run. Twice. The genre of mistake is consistent.

The same stretch trimmed the cost where it had crept; the daily article summaries had been quietly using the expensive model, so I moved them to a cheaper one that does the job just as well and roughly halved the monthly spend. Boring, satisfying, the kind of thing that only matters because nobody's watching it.

12 JuneFriday · today

An honest second opinion

With the thing finally complete, I had a separate model review the entire codebase cold, told to be skeptical and specific. It was a good call. It found a handful of real, quiet erosions; ways the system could double-count if I ever changed the rules, a way a failed write could silently mark an item as done and lose it, and a genuinely sharp catch on the brand-new code, where an expired login could let a truncated article slip in unnoticed. None of it was on fire. All of it was the kind of slow data-quality leak that costs you later. I fixed the ones worth fixing the same afternoon and wrote down the two that need a proper migration to do safely.

Getting a second model to attack your own work, with no ego in it, is one of the genuinely new things about building this way. It doesn't get tired or defensive, and it doesn't care that you just declared yourself finished.

What the five weeks actually taught me

Claude wrote almost all of the code, which is the least interesting thing about the whole project. What Claude could not do was decide what counted. It had no opinion on whether a wry one-liner was a real short or a joke, on how much to trust the crowd against the writer, on when "this aged well" is a position and when it's a postcard. It would happily extract a signal from a shrug if I let it. Every threshold in the thing is a judgement I had to make and then defend against the system's own eagerness to find meaning everywhere.

So the division of labour was clean and a little surprising: AI made iteration almost free, and taste still had to come from somewhere. The taste came out of a lot of imperfect reactions; this is noise, that's real, you're over-reading, you're attributing the wrong person, miss it rather than fake it. That loop was the work. The fumbles in this journal are not incidental to it; they are it. The good version of building with AI isn't fewer mistakes, it's cheaper ones, made faster, with Claude to say the obvious thing back to you when you've talked yourself somewhere silly.

It replaced a habit I was never going to win; reading several chats and several article streams every day and trusting myself, at midnight, with eleven tabs open, to notice the one line that mattered. A quieter version of that attention now runs without me and hands me the shortlist. It isn't always right. It's reliably less wrong than me at midnight, and on the mornings it surfaces something I'd have walked straight past, it pays for the whole month of subscriptions in a single message.

A note on sources. This reads publications I personally pay for, for my own use, and I've deliberately kept every name out of it; the publications, the writers, the tickers, all of it. The system is the point, not the people I read. This journal is the first of a few; I'm planning to document the other things I've built the same way, dates and dead-ends included.
