Building / proof-of-work

Building chatpulse

A small system that reads everything I pay to read, and tells me what's worth my attention.

I subscribe to more investing newsletters than I can actually read. Each one is good; that's the problem. They come with member chats that run all day, articles that land without warning, and the quiet anxiety that the one thing worth knowing got said at 2am in a thread I scrolled past. I was paying for an edge and spending it on keeping up.

So I built a thing that keeps up for me. It reads every message and every article across the handful of paid sources I follow, works out who is actually saying something about which company and how strongly they mean it, and surfaces the parts where independent voices start agreeing. It runs on a Mac Mini under my desk; it costs a few cents a day; and it has quietly become the first thing I check.

I'm not a developer. I'm a market researcher who spent years turning messy human signal into something a business could act on, which, it turns out, is most of what this is. The hard part was never the code. It was deciding what counts as signal.

From a message to a signal

A chat is mostly noise; jokes, "gm", screenshots, someone asking what everyone thinks of a name. The job is to find the few lines where a person is actually taking a position, and to be honest about how much they meant it. A model reads each message and each article and returns the same boring shape every time: which company, is this buy or sell or just watching, how convinced, and the exact words that justify it. If there's no real view, it says so and moves on. Silence is a valid answer; a system that invents signal is worse than one that stays quiet.

every message gets read the same way, and reduced to the same handful of fields

Two details took the longest to get right, and both are judgement, not engineering. The first is telling apart "I just bought this" from "I bought this months ago and it worked out"; the second sounds like conviction and is really a memory. The second is telling apart a stance from a reaction; "thoughts on this name?" mentions a company without holding any view of it. Most of my time went into teaching the system to under-react: to leave things out rather than dress up a shrug as a signal.

Convergence, not volume

The mistake would be to rank by how often a name gets mentioned; that just surfaces whatever's loud. What I care about is the opposite; the moment several people who don't talk to each other start landing on the same name, in the same direction, around the same time. Quiet agreement across independent voices is the thing a pile of subscriptions is supposed to buy you, and it's exactly the thing you miss when you're reading them one tab at a time.

whose voice it is matters as much as what they said

Push, and pull

For a while it only pushed; one synthesised message each morning, and short live nudges during the day when a writer opened a position or a quiet name suddenly heated up. That covered the "tell me what I missed" half. The half I underrated was being able to ask back. So it grew a second mouth: a small assistant I can message and interrogate. "Why is this name showing up?" returns who said what, with their actual words, even for names too thinly covered to ever make the morning brief. Push tells me what it decided was important; pull lets me check everything it didn't.

The mess

None of this arrived clean. I spent the better part of an evening convinced I'd found a deep bug; the system kept reporting that it had processed hundreds of items while saving none of them. I read the code three times. The cause was a flag I'd left off my own command; it had been doing exactly what I told it, which was nothing. Another night went to a networking detour I talked myself into because I'd misremembered which machine was whose; the fix, once I stopped being clever, was a thirty-second login. The actual engineering was mostly fine. The errors were all me being confidently slightly wrong, which is the genre of mistake I make most.

There's a cost discipline running underneath it too. Reading every message with a top-tier model would be lovely and would also quietly bleed money, so the cheap, boring model does the millions of small reads and the expensive one is saved for the one thing a human actually consumes; the morning synthesis. Watching the daily spend sit at a few cents is its own small satisfaction.

What AI did, and didn't

AI wrote most of the code, which is the least interesting thing about it. What it could not do was decide what counted. It had no opinion on whether a wry one-liner was a real short or just a joke, on how much to trust the crowd versus the author, on when "this aged well" is a position and when it's a postcard. It would happily extract a signal from a shrug if I let it. Every threshold in the thing is a judgement I had to make and then defend against my own system's eagerness to find meaning. The model made the work cheap; the taste, again, had to come from somewhere.

It replaced an unwinnable daily habit; reading several chats and several article streams and trusting myself to notice the one line that mattered. Now a quieter version of that attention runs without me, and hands me the shortlist. It is not always right. It is reliably less wrong than me at midnight with eleven open tabs, and on the days it surfaces something I'd have walked past, it pays for the whole month of subscriptions in a single notification.

A note on sources: this reads publications I pay for, for my own use. I've kept every name out of this on purpose; the system is the point, not the people I read.
